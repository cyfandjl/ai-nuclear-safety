Three People. One Question.

Before the policy briefs and technical jargon, meet three everyday people wrestling with the same question as nuclear regulators. Their stories are composites — drawn from real interviews, public hearings, and community surveys — but their concerns are very real.

This issue isn’t abstract. Whether AI helps license the next generation of reactors will shape the air we breathe, the price of electricity, and the kind of planet we leave behind. These voices represent the diverse stakes most of us have in the outcome.

👩‍👦‍👧
Maya, 38
Mother & Nurse · Ohio

“I grew up hearing the word ‘Chernobyl’ like a warning.”

Maya lives 20 miles from an aging reactor. When she heard regulators might use AI to speed up license renewals, her first reaction was fear. “A computer can’t smell something burning. It can’t panic when the numbers look wrong.” But her cousin, an ICU nurse, reminded her that AI already flags sepsis faster than doctors do — and saves lives.

Now she’s torn. She wants cleaner energy for her kids’ asthma. She also wants someone human to be accountable if something goes wrong at 3 a.m.

Her question: Who do I call when an algorithm makes the wrong call?
👨‍🏭
Robert, 67
Retired Nuclear Engineer · South Carolina

“We spent 14 years licensing one reactor. That can’t be the future.”

Robert watched the Vogtle 3 and 4 reactors balloon from $14 billion to over $30 billion. He believes the U.S. nuclear industry is dying not from physics, but from paperwork. “I’ve seen engineers spend six months on one safety analysis report that AI could draft in a day.”

He trusts AI — cautiously — because he’s seen what sloppy automation looks like at Boeing. The difference, he says, is that nuclear has 70 years of safety culture. Done right, AI could free engineers to think rather than type.

His question: Can we automate the boring parts without losing the wisdom?
🌿
Sofia, 22
College Student & Climate Organizer · California

“My generation doesn’t have 14 years to wait.”

Sofia used to be firmly anti-nuclear — until she ran the numbers for a college project. She realized wind and solar alone couldn’t replace coal fast enough to meet the 2035 climate targets she’d been marching for. “If AI can shave five years off a reactor license, that’s five fewer years of coal burning in the atmosphere my niece will breathe.”

Still, she doesn’t want Silicon Valley deciding nuclear safety with a black-box model trained on who-knows-what data. She wants open science, open code, and open hearings.

Her question: How do we move fast and stay transparent?

Key Concepts to Understand

These foundational ideas shape every section that follows. Spend a moment here first — the futures cone, case studies, and guardrails will make much more sense once you know the vocabulary.

🌱

What Is a "Wicked Problem"?

AI in nuclear safety is a wicked problem — a challenge with no single right answer, where solutions create new problems, stakeholders disagree on definitions, and every attempt to fix it changes the problem itself. Unlike "tame" problems (e.g., solving an equation), wicked problems involve deep uncertainty, competing values (safety vs. speed vs. cost), and consequences that span generations. This project maps that complexity rather than pretending simple answers exist.

Foundational
🔍

The Futures Cone Framework

The futures cone is a foresight tool that visualises multiple possible futures radiating out from the present. Rather than predicting one outcome, it maps what is possible (could happen), plausible (could reasonably happen), probable (likely to happen), and preferable (what we want to happen). This framework forces us to consider uncertainty seriously and plan for multiple outcomes — essential for nuclear safety governance spanning decades.

Framework
👥

Human-AI Teaming in Safety-Critical Systems

A central tension throughout this project: how much authority should AI have vs. human operators? The Three Mile Island, Chernobyl, and Boeing 737 MAX cases show that both pure human control and pure automation can fail catastrophically. The emerging consensus is that AI and humans must work as a team, each compensating for the other's weaknesses — but designing that partnership is one of the hardest challenges in nuclear AI governance.

Core Debate
🛠

Defence-in-Depth Principle

Nuclear safety relies on multiple independent layers of protection so that no single failure causes disaster. AI adds a new layer to this concept: it can serve as an additional safety barrier (detecting anomalies humans miss) but also introduces a new type of failure mode (algorithmic errors, adversarial attacks, data poisoning). The question is whether AI strengthens or weakens defence-in-depth — and the answer depends entirely on how it's implemented and governed.

Safety Principle
📈

Why This Matters Now

We are at a pivotal moment (2025–2026): the NRC has published its AI Strategic Plan, the DOE has demonstrated AI licensing acceleration, and the nuclear industry is actively deploying AI tools. Decisions made in the next few years will shape nuclear safety governance for decades. Understanding these issues isn't just academic — it's about informed citizenship in an era where AI is being embedded into the systems that protect public safety.

Past → Today: Cases Where AI Changed Everything

Before looking forward, we must understand how AI has already shaped safety-critical industries — and what nuclear can learn from these pivotal moments. Click any card to expand.

1979

Three Mile Island — The Case for Smarter Alarms

Lesson
The partial meltdown at TMI-2 was caused in large part by operators misinterpreting over 100 simultaneous alarms. No intelligent system existed to prioritise warnings or diagnose the root cause in real-time.
Studies by INPO later showed that roughly 48% of nuclear plant incidents between 2010–2011 traced back to human error. TMI demonstrated the catastrophic cost of information overload without intelligent filtering — exactly the problem AI anomaly detection now aims to solve. Modern AI alarm-prioritisation systems can correlate thousands of signals in milliseconds, identifying the true root cause and suppressing cascading false alarms.
Nuclear AI Lesson: AI-driven alarm management and operator support systems could prevent the exact type of cognitive overload that led to TMI. This became a foundational argument for AI adoption in reactor control rooms.
🔗 Source · NRC Three Mile Island Fact Sheet

1986

Chernobyl — When Human Override Defeats Safety

Lesson
Operators deliberately disabled safety systems during a test, and flawed reactor design allowed a power excursion. Approximately 70% of errors in nuclear operations are attributed to human factors.
Chernobyl exposed the danger of allowing humans to override safety interlocks without intelligent safeguards. An AI system monitoring reactor physics in real-time would have detected the dangerous power configuration and could have provided hard limits that operators could not bypass without multi-level authorisation. This disaster fundamentally reshaped thinking about defence-in-depth and became a reference case for why AI-human teaming, rather than pure human control, may be safer.
Nuclear AI Lesson: AI can serve as an independent safety layer that detects dangerous operating regimes and prevents unsafe manual overrides — but the question of how much authority to give AI vs. human operators remains central to nuclear AI governance.
🔗 Source · IAEA Chornobyl Accident Overview

2011

Fukushima Daiichi — Failure of Imagination

Lesson
A beyond-design-basis tsunami overwhelmed defences that had not been updated despite available evidence. The disaster highlighted failures in probabilistic risk assessment and scenario planning.
Carnegie Endowment research argued Fukushima was preventable had regulators and operators incorporated available seismic and tsunami evidence into risk models. Modern AI-powered probabilistic risk assessment tools can continuously ingest new geological data, climate models, and historical event databases to dynamically update risk estimates — something static, human-reviewed assessments failed to do at Fukushima. AI-driven digital twins now model extreme scenarios that fall outside traditional engineering assumptions.
Nuclear AI Lesson: AI excels at continuously updating risk models with new data — precisely the capability that was missing before Fukushima. But AI models are only as good as their training data, and truly unprecedented events ("unknown unknowns") remain a challenge.
🔗 Source · Carnegie Endowment · Why Fukushima Was Preventable

2016

DeepMind AlphaGo — AI Surpasses Human Judgment

Opportunity
DeepMind's AlphaGo defeated the world Go champion, demonstrating that AI could master complex pattern recognition and strategic reasoning beyond human capability in constrained domains.
This breakthrough ignited serious discussion in safety-critical industries: if AI can outperform humans in complex decision spaces, could it do the same for reactor safety monitoring? DeepMind later applied similar techniques to reduce Google data centre cooling energy by 40% — a direct analogue to nuclear plant optimisation. The nuclear community began exploring whether deep reinforcement learning could optimise reactor operations within safety constraints.
Nuclear AI Lesson: AI can identify patterns and optimal strategies invisible to humans, but nuclear safety requires explainability that games do not — you cannot accept "trust me" from an AI controlling a reactor. This tension drove demand for Explainable AI (XAI) in nuclear applications.
🔗 Source · DeepMind AlphaGo

2018

Boeing 737 MAX MCAS — Automation Catastrophe

Risk
Two crashes killed 346 people when the MCAS automated system, relying on a single sensor, repeatedly pushed aircraft into nosedives. Pilots were unaware MCAS existed. Boeing's cost was $20+ billion.
The MCAS disaster became the defining cautionary tale for AI in safety-critical systems. Key failures directly relevant to nuclear AI included: reliance on a single sensor input (no redundancy), an automated system that could override human operators, deliberate concealment of the system's existence from pilots and regulators, and a regulatory body (FAA) that delegated too much oversight to the manufacturer. The NRC explicitly studied the Boeing 737 MAX crashes for lessons applicable to digital instrumentation and control in nuclear plants.
Nuclear AI Lesson: The NRC published a dedicated analysis of Boeing 737 MAX crashes for nuclear digital I&C lessons. Key takeaways: never rely on single-point data sources, ensure operators understand AI system behaviour, maintain regulatory independence from industry self-certification, and design for graceful degradation.
🔗 Source · U.S. House Committee · Boeing 737 MAX Investigation

2018

Uber Self-Driving Fatality — When AI Safety Backup Fails

Risk
An Uber autonomous vehicle killed a pedestrian in Arizona. The AI detected the person 6 seconds before impact but failed to classify the object correctly, and the human safety driver was inattentive.
This case exposed the "automation complacency" problem: when AI handles most situations well, human monitors become less vigilant over time. The safety driver was watching a video on her phone. For nuclear applications, this directly challenges the "human-in-the-loop" model — if operators trust AI to handle routine monitoring, will they remain alert enough to catch the rare cases where AI fails? The incident also showed how AI classification errors in edge cases can have fatal consequences.
Nuclear AI Lesson: "Human-in-the-loop" is necessary but not sufficient. Nuclear AI systems must be designed to actively engage operators, not just passively rely on them to catch errors. Automation complacency is a real threat that must be designed against.
🔗 Source · NTSB Uber Tempe Investigation

2013–2023

IBM Watson for Oncology — The $4B AI Failure

Risk
IBM spent $4 billion on Watson for healthcare, promising AI-driven cancer treatment recommendations. It was discontinued in 2023 after providing unsafe recommendations and failing to generalise beyond its training institution.
Watson was trained primarily on protocols from Memorial Sloan Kettering in the US, making its recommendations inappropriate for different clinical contexts globally. Reports emerged of Watson suggesting unsafe treatment plans. The fundamental problem: AI trained on narrow data cannot be trusted for safety-critical decisions in varied real-world contexts. For nuclear, this directly parallels concerns about AI trained on one reactor type being applied to different designs, or AI trained on US regulatory frameworks being used internationally.
Nuclear AI Lesson: AI safety tools must be validated against the specific context they'll operate in — not just the data they were trained on. A model trained on one reactor fleet may fail dangerously on another. Domain-specific validation and ongoing monitoring are essential.
🔗 Source · STAT News · 4 Lessons from IBM Watson Health

2020

ARPA-E GEMINA — $27M Bet on Nuclear Digital Twins

Opportunity
The DOE's ARPA-E launched the GEMINA program, funding 9 projects with $27 million to develop AI-enabled predictive maintenance digital twins for advanced nuclear reactors.
GEMINA (Generating Electricity Managed by Intelligent Nuclear Assets) represented the US government's first major investment specifically in AI-nuclear integration. Projects included Exelon partnering with GE Research to build digital twins of the BWRX-300 SMR, and EDF developing turbo-generator digital twins using support vector machines for fault prediction. These digital twins continuously learn from sensor data to predict equipment health in real-time, enabling predictive rather than time-based maintenance. Early results showed potential to significantly reduce operations and maintenance costs.
Nuclear AI Lesson: Government-funded programs like GEMINA demonstrate that AI-nuclear integration works best as a collaborative effort between national labs, utilities, and technology companies — with clear safety objectives and structured validation requirements.
🔗 Source · ARPA-E GEMINA Program

2020

AlphaFold — AI Solves 50-Year Science Problem

Opportunity
DeepMind's AlphaFold predicted protein structures with lab-quality accuracy, solving a grand challenge of biology. It demonstrated AI's power to accelerate scientific breakthroughs that previously seemed decades away.
AlphaFold showed that AI can make transformative contributions to complex scientific domains when properly trained and validated. For nuclear, this inspired optimism that AI could similarly crack difficult problems in reactor physics, materials science for extreme environments, and long-term waste behaviour modelling. The key difference: AlphaFold's predictions could be independently verified through lab experiments, whereas nuclear safety AI predictions about rare catastrophic events cannot be experimentally validated.
Nuclear AI Lesson: AI can accelerate nuclear science dramatically, but nuclear safety requires a higher validation bar than most scientific domains. We cannot "experimentally verify" an AI's prediction about a reactor meltdown scenario — making simulation fidelity and conservative assumptions critical.
🔗 Source · AlphaFold Protein Structure Database

2025

DOE AI Licensing Demo — Weeks to Hours

Opportunity
The Department of Energy demonstrated AI completing nuclear licensing document reviews in a single day that previously took human teams 4–6 weeks, with improved accuracy and consistency.
This landmark demonstration by the Nuclear Regulatory Infrastructure Committee (NRIC) proved that AI could achieve a 50% reduction in both document development time and regulatory review cycles while simultaneously improving accuracy, consistency, and traceability. Argonne National Laboratory developed companion tools to help applicants prepare more complete submissions. However, critics including the AI Now Institute warned that speed should not compromise the thoroughness that nuclear licensing demands. The NRC emphasised AI serves as an "analytical layer" supporting human experts, not replacing their judgment.
Nuclear AI Lesson: AI can dramatically accelerate nuclear licensing while maintaining quality — but the balance between efficiency and rigour must be continuously monitored. This is the pivotal moment where AI moves from experimental to operational in nuclear regulation.
🔗 Source · DOE · AI to Reduce Reactor Licensing Timelines

2025

NRC AI Strategic Plan — Regulatory Framework Takes Shape

Mixed
The NRC published its comprehensive AI Strategic Plan and conducted a regulatory gap assessment for AI technologies, establishing the foundation for governing AI in nuclear applications.
The NRC's Strategic Plan addresses both internal AI use (for regulatory efficiency) and external AI use (by industry in nuclear facilities). It acknowledged that existing regulatory frameworks may have gaps for AI technologies and committed to developing guidance. The agency invested in foundational AI tools and hosted public workshops. However, some experts warned the framework may already be lagging behind rapid AI advancement, and that the NRC faces pressure to be both an AI adopter and an AI regulator — a potential conflict of interest.
Nuclear AI Lesson: Establishing governance before widespread deployment is crucial, but regulatory frameworks must be adaptive enough to keep pace with AI evolution. The dual role of regulator-as-user creates governance challenges that need transparent management.
🔗 Source · NRC NUREG-2261 AI Strategic Plan

2022–2026

Generative AI Revolution — New Capabilities, New Risks

Mixed
The explosion of large language models (ChatGPT, Claude, etc.) created powerful new tools for document analysis and knowledge management, while raising novel concerns about hallucination and misuse.
Generative AI offered nuclear regulators powerful new capabilities: summarising thousands of pages of technical documents, cross-referencing regulatory requirements, drafting safety analyses, and creating training materials. But these models can "hallucinate" — generating plausible-sounding but incorrect information with high confidence. In safety-critical nuclear contexts, a confidently wrong AI answer could be catastrophic. The NRC and DOE began specifically studying how to harness generative AI benefits while protecting against its unique failure modes, including automated fact-checking and human verification workflows.
Nuclear AI Lesson: Generative AI is simultaneously the most promising and most dangerous AI technology for nuclear safety. Its ability to process and synthesise vast regulatory knowledge is unmatched, but its tendency to hallucinate demands rigorous verification systems that add complexity back into the workflow.
🔗 Source · NIST AI Safety & Trustworthiness

Recommended Guardrails

Essential safeguards for responsible AI deployment in nuclear safety and licensing.

Human-in-the-Loop

All AI outputs in safety-critical contexts must be reviewed and approved by qualified human experts. AI serves as an analytical layer, not a decision-maker. Final licensing authority must remain with human regulators.

🔗 Source · NRC AI Strategic Plan (NUREG-2261)

Explainability & Transparency

AI models used in regulatory processes must provide interpretable reasoning paths. Black-box models should not be used for safety analysis without validated explanation methods and comprehensive documentation.

🔗 Source · NIST AI Risk Management Framework

Independent V&V Testing

AI systems must undergo rigorous independent verification and validation (V&V), including adversarial testing, edge-case analysis, and performance benchmarking against established safety standards like the NIST AI RMF.

🔗 Source · NRC Digital I&C Research

Cybersecurity Hardening

AI systems in nuclear facilities require security-by-design principles, continuous monitoring for adversarial attacks, data poisoning, and model manipulation across both safety and non-safety applications.

🔗 Source · NRC Cyber Security Program

Regulatory Independence

AI adoption must not undermine the independence of nuclear regulators. Efficiency gains should complement, not replace, the rigor of safety oversight. Political pressure to accelerate should not override safety standards.

🔗 Source · IAEA Nuclear Safety & Security

Continuous Monitoring & Audit

Deployed AI systems require ongoing performance monitoring, periodic audits, and revalidation as operating conditions change. Comprehensive audit trails must be maintained for regulatory accountability.

🔗 Source · DOE · AI Tools for Nuclear Fleet Monitoring
